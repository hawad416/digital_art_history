Thread 1: Regional Granularity (EDITING STILL)
Within a single country, do city-level floral patterns reflect distinct cultural histories?
The central finding in Thread 1 is that country-level data masks enormous variation. Two regions within the same country can look completely opposite in their motif composition. These splits map closely onto historical boundaries, Habsburg vs. Venetian influence in Croatia, Ottoman vs. Serbian heartland in Serbia, supporting what the Blanche Payne archive documents say directly: “People identified mostly with their historic regions or lands until the nineteenth century, when the development of capitalism and integration processes caused the birth of modern national identities.”

A key factor shaping these regional differences was centuries of different imperial rule. Until World War I, Austria-Hungary governed present-day Slovenia, Croatia, and Bosnia and Herzegovina,2 while the Ottoman Empire held Serbia and Montenegro until 1878 and Macedonia as late as 1912.1 These separate political spheres produced distinct cultural traditions that outlasted the empires themselves. Even after these populations were brought under a single Yugoslav government during Payne's research trips in the 1930s, the archive notes they “kept their cultural traditions alive through a revived interest in dance and costume”1 and textile motifs continued to differ sharply from region to region. Our data reflects exactly this: the same country can contain regions that look completely opposite in their motif composition, because they were never culturally unified to begin with. 1 Blanche Payne Regional Costume Photograph and Drawing Collection, Archives West, Historical Background and Yugoslav Regional Costume sections. 2 "Austro-Hungarian Rule in Bosnia and Herzegovina." Croatia 167 items · 5 regions analyzed

Posavina (inland, Sava River valley) fell under Habsburg rule, while Dalmatia (Adriatic coast) was shaped by centuries of Venetian and Mediterranean contact. These two regions sit at the extreme ends of every motif comparison in the dataset. Baranja and Slavonski Brod sit in between, as does Zagreb and Šestine. Zagreb's traditional folk costume is actually named after Šestine, the highland village on the city's outskirts where the tradition originated, which is why the two are treated as a single entry in the dataset.2 2 Jozanović, Nikolina. "The Most Valuable Hallmark of Zagreb." LoveZagreb.hr. Accessed 2026. https://www.lovezagreb.hr/topics/more-than-words/the-most-valuable-hallmark-of-zagreb/

The archive notes broadly that interaction with other countries shaped “costume production, materials and motifs”1 across Yugoslavia. In Croatia, that interaction produced two almost entirely different textile traditions within a single modern country. The 65 percentage point gap between Posavina and Dalmatia is, we would argue, a direct reflection of that history. 1 Blanche Payne Regional Costume Photograph and Drawing Collection, Archives West, Yugoslav Regional Costume section.

Reading the Croatia data

Posavina's 80% floral rate is not driven by one garment type. Kerchiefs are 90% floral, caps 65%, aprons 80%, belts 78%. Floral is the consistent aesthetic across every garment in Posavina. Dalmatia's 15% rate is equally consistent in the opposite direction. All 61 Posavina items are women's garments; Dalmatia includes 11 men's items (9% floral).

Serbia 111 items · Kosovo and Sumadija analyzed

Kosovo-Metohije and Sumadija share a country but not a history. The archive documents that the Ottoman Empire held Serbia and Montenegro until 1878, while Kosovo remained under Ottoman control until 19121 meaning these two Serbian regions had significantly different histories of outside cultural influence. Meanwhile, the archive describes a 19th-century resurgence of interest in regional dress that “coincided with a romanticized and idealized idea of the peasant past, which culminated in the idea of ‘our dress,’ emblematic of national identity.”1 Sumadija, as a center of Serbian cultural identity, likely concentrated that revival more intensely than border regions with more complex political histories. Our data shows the result: Kosovo-Metohije at 18% floral, Sumadija at 46%. Blanche Payne collected during King Alexander's dictatorship (her 1930 trip) and its turbulent aftermath (1936–37). 1 Blanche Payne Regional Costume Photograph and Drawing Collection, Archives West, Historical Background section.

The Serbia anomaly: men's stockings

Sumadija's high floral rate (46%) is almost entirely driven by its 12 stockings, every one 100% floral. Kosovo has zero men's items in the dataset. Just because the Henry holds 12 elaborately embroidered Sumadija stockings does not mean all Serbian men wore floral stockings. We hypothesize that these were likely festival pieces that caught a Payne's eye. This is the curatorial argument: what Blanche chose to collect shapes every finding we can make.

Macedonia 178 items · 7 regions analyzed

Macedonia was under Ottoman rule until 1912 which was far later than Croatia or northern Serbia. We believe its textile tradition reflects that: the collection is predominantly geometric across all regions. Sub-regional variation exists, with Mariovo and Pelagonija Bitola reaching 40–45% floral, while Pelagonija Bitola-Prilep sits at 12%. Note: Gostivar's 14 items are all stockings. Its rate reflects one garment type, not a regional aesthetic.

Macedonia's complexity

Unlike Croatia, Macedonia does not have a clean high/low split. Most regions sit between 12–45% floral with heavy geometric dominance throughout. The more interesting story in Macedonia is what is absent: the Henry has almost no men's items from Macedonia outside of Gostivar stockings. Whether this reflects Blanche's access, the nature of male dress in the region, or curatorial selection is an open question this dataset cannot answer.

Same garment, different region Apron floral rate across regions

The apron is the most common garment type (n=94). Comparing its floral rate across regions reveals that region predicts motif more powerfully than garment type. The same item looks completely different depending on where it came from.

Methodology & exclusions

All bars show the 500 classified rows only. “Floral inclusion rate” counts any item where floral = yes, including items tagged both floral and geometric. Regions with fewer than 10 items are excluded from main visualizations. Bosnia-Herzegovina (n=29 total, largest sub-region n=7) is not analyzed at the regional level.
